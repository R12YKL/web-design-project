What Is Applied Mathematics?

Applied Mathematics is the part of mathematics that focuses on using maths to model and solve practical problems.

Instead of only studying abstract ideas, applied maths connects formulas, graphs, equations, and logical thinking to situations from the real world.

It is used in areas such as physics, computer science, engineering, economics, and data analysis.

Why Applied Maths Matters

Applied maths helps us describe change, predict outcomes, and understand patterns.

It is useful for solving problems involving speed, growth, forces, probability, optimisation, and decision-making.

In computing, applied maths is important because many algorithms, simulations, and models rely on mathematical thinking.

Main Topics In Applied Maths

Applied maths often includes topics such as algebra, trigonometry, calculus, statistics, probability, and mathematical modelling.

These topics help describe quantities, movement, uncertainty, and relationships between variables.

A model is a mathematical way of representing a real-life situation so that it can be studied more clearly.

Applied Maths In Real Life

Engineers use applied maths to design structures and machines.

Scientists use it to describe motion, energy, and physical systems.

Businesses use it to study profits, costs, trends, and risk.

Computer scientists use it in graphics, artificial intelligence, cryptography, machine learning, and simulations.

Example Of A Simple Formula

One common example in applied maths is the formula for speed: 

speed = distance / time

This formula helps describe motion and shows how mathematics can be used to understand something practical.

Another example is the area of a rectangle: 

area = length × width

These formulas show that applied maths connects numbers to real measurements and situations.

How To Study Applied Maths

The best way to study applied maths is to understand the meaning of each formula, not just memorise it.

Practice solving questions step by step, draw diagrams when needed, and always check what the variables represent.

It also helps to connect each topic to a real-life example so the maths feels more meaningful.
